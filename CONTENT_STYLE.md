# Content Style Guide
21/07/26 v1

Governs writing style for every Digital Practice Card and Resource in this
repository. Applies to `underlying_needs`, `digital_practices` descriptions,
`learning_lenses` content, and every field in a Resource's `how_to` section.
Referenced by `AI_CONSTITUTION.md` — any builder session touching content
must read this file first.

This does not change scope. Section 3 of the Blueprint still governs what
content is allowed to say. This governs how it's said.

---

## Who this is for

Assume the reader has low confidence with digital tools. This is true for
every layer, but it is the entire reason Digital Foundations exists — write
Foundations content as if it may be the person's first time doing this at
all. Do not assume they know where a button lives, what an icon means, or
what a tool is called before you tell them.

This is not about writing down to anyone. It's about removing every point
where someone could get stuck, feel stupid, and quietly give up.

---

## Rules

**1. Define every tool on first use.**
Don't write "use the Immersive Reader." Write "Immersive Reader — a
built-in Microsoft tool that reads text aloud and simplifies the page —
can be turned on from..." A term can be used freely after it's been
introduced once on that page, not before.

**2. No unexplained jargon or acronyms.**
If a term is unavoidable (WCAG, GDPR, SEND), expand it in plain words the
first time it appears on the page. Don't assume prior knowledge of any
platform, framework, or internal shorthand.

**3. Step-by-step means literal steps, not a summary.**
Weak: "Set up your channels for the term."
Correct: "1. Open Teams. 2. Select your class team from the left-hand
list. 3. Select the + at the top of the channel list. 4. Name the
channel..."
If a step involves clicking something, name exactly what it's called and
roughly where it is ("top-right of the screen," "left-hand menu").

**4. Say why before how, briefly.**
One sentence on why this is worth doing, before the steps. Not a
justification essay — a reason to keep reading.

**5. Short sentences. One idea per sentence.**
If a sentence has more than one "and," split it.

**6. Foundations gets the most literal version.**
Digital Inclusion and Digital Innovation can assume someone is
comfortable navigating Microsoft 365 generally. Foundations cannot —
write as if this is the reader's first time opening the app in question.

**7. Warm, not brisk.**
This is coaching, not a manual. A short, human line is fine ("Once this
is set up, you won't need to touch it again this term") — but keep it
to one line, don't pad.

**8. Never imply the reader should already know this.**
No "simply," "just," "obviously," or "of course." These words tell a
low-confidence reader they're behind. Cut them without exception.

## Before / after example

**Before (too brief, jargon-heavy):**
> Enable captions in Stream for accessibility. Check the transcript is
> accurate before publishing.

**After:**
> Captions let learners read along with a video — essential for anyone
> who's deaf or hard of hearing, and genuinely useful for everyone else
> too. Here's how to turn them on:
> 1. Open your video in Stream.
> 2. Select the three dots (⋯) above the video and choose **Edit video
>    details**.
> 3. Go to the **Captions** tab and select **Generate automatically**.
> 4. Once it's generated, read through the caption text and fix any
>    mistakes — automatic captions get names and technical terms wrong
>    fairly often.
> 5. Select **Publish** once you're happy with it.
> Skipping the check in step 4 is the most common mistake — an inaccurate
> transcript can be more confusing than no captions at all.

## What this does NOT mean

- It does not mean padding content with filler to look thorough.
- It does not mean explaining Windows or Microsoft 365 from first
  principles inside every card — that's what the device-basics Resources
  are for. Cards link out to those rather than re-explaining them.
- It does not loosen the Section 3 boundary. Plain language about a
  digital tool is always in scope. Plain language about a teaching
  decision is still out of scope, however gently it's phrased.
